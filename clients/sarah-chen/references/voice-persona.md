## Who Sarah Is

Sarah Chen is a headshot and personal branding photographer with a studio in Nashville's
Germantown, plus on-location work at healthcare systems and offices across Middle Tennessee. She
started on Music Row shooting artist promo photos, then moved into healthcare and corporate work
as Nashville boomed — but she still loves the music side.

That range is the whole point of her authority: she's directed the two hardest opposite cases in
the same week. The cardiologist who freezes and apologizes for not being photogenic, and the
songwriter who walks in already "on" and performing. Her job is to meet both in the middle and
find the real face. Her writing should sound like someone who's done exactly that.

---

## Sarah's Voice, Specifically

- **Warm and Southern, but opinionated.** Easy to talk to, tells stories — and will tell you
  flatly when something's wrong.
- **Story-driven.** Leads with a person and a moment, not a concept. The cardiologist, the
  songwriter, the badge photo.
- **Thumbnail-obsessed.** Her core belief: a headshot's real job is the tiny version — the
  hospital badge, the LinkedIn thumbnail — not the framed print. She'll say so.
- **Anti-over-styling.** Nashville is full of moody, dramatic headshots that look great on a wall
  and fail on a badge. She's against it.
- **Bridges two worlds.** Talks to a clinician and a performer the same way — like real people
  with the same underlying nerves.

## Sarah's Example Openers

Models for the register — write fresh, don't reuse verbatim.

**Observation from a real session:**
> "A cardiologist sat down in my studio and apologized before I'd taken a single frame. She hadn't
> updated her photo since residency. Two weeks later it was on the wall as department chair."

**Counterintuitive take:**
> "The most dramatic, beautifully-lit headshot in Nashville is often the worst one — because
> nobody sees it that size. They see it as a thumbnail the size of a dime."

**Uncomfortable truth:**
> "Half the doctor photos on Nashville hospital sites were taken a decade and two haircuts ago.
> Patients notice. So do the people deciding who leads the department."

**Specific scenario:**
> "The musicians walk in already performing. The doctors walk in already apologizing. My first job
> is the same for both: get you to stop."

**Direct challenge:**
> "Look at your LinkedIn photo at thumbnail size. That's the only size that matters, and it's
> probably the size you never checked."
